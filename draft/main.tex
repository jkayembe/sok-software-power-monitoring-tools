\paragraph{} In this paper, we present a Systematisation of Knowledge (SoK) on software power and energy measurement tools. While we do not aim for an exhaustive survey, our goal is to organise existing tools and approaches along dimensions that capture their main design choices, operational trade-offs, and practical applicability.

More specifically, we systematise profiling tools according to dimensions including:
\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Granularity}: what software entities are attributed energy consumption,

    \item \textbf{Measurement sources}: how energy and activity information are obtained,

    \item \textbf{Attribution mechanisms}: how system-level energy is distributed across software entities,

    \item \textbf{Virtualised environment support}: how profiling is supported in virtualised and cloud-native infrastructures,

    \item \textbf{Operational constraints}: runtime overhead, temporal resolution, privilege requirements, portability, and instrumentation constraints,

    \item \textbf{Maturity and ecosystem}: deployment complexity, validation, maintenance, documentation, and ecosystem integration.

\end{itemize}

This systematisation exposes common patterns, implicit assumptions, and trade-offs that are often obscured when tools are considered individually.

\paragraph{} The contributions of this SoK are threefold. First, we propose a taxonomy of software energy measurement approaches that clarifies the design space and provides a shared vocabulary for future work. Secondly, we derive a decision framework that supports researchers and practitioners in selecting measurement tools based on their experimental goals and operational constraints. Third, through a comparative analysis of representative tools, we identify open challenges and research gaps. By consolidating and structuring existing knowledge, this paper aims to serve as a foundation for both empirical research and tool development in software energy measurement.


\section{Profiling Challenges} \label{challenges}

Energy profiling of software systems is inherently indirect. While hardware components consume energy, software only influences this consumption through its use of system resources. As a result, profiling tools do not measure software energy consumption directly, but instead estimate and attribute it based on observable signals and modelling assumptions.

Most approaches can be abstracted as a combination of two sources of information:  
(i) system-level energy estimations, obtained from hardware interfaces, external measurements, or models, and  
(ii) resource utilisation information, describing how software entities use shared hardware resources over time.

Profiling consists in combining these sources to reconstruct an attribution of energy consumption to software entities. This process raises several fundamental challenges.

\subsection{Energy Attribution and Allocation}

The primary challenge is allocation, i.e., determining how system-level energy consumption should be distributed across software entities.

\paragraph{Granularity of attribution}

Attribution can be performed at different levels:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Process-level}, using operating system metrics to distribute energy across processes,
    \item \textbf{Thread-level}, providing finer visibility within processes,
    \item \textbf{Code-level}, where energy is attributed to functions or methods.
\end{itemize}

Increasing granularity improves interpretability but requires stronger assumptions and additional instrumentation.

\paragraph{Attribution mechanisms}

The choice of attribution mechanism is tightly coupled with the targeted granularity.

\begin{itemize}
    \item At \textbf{process or thread level}, attribution is typically based on \textbf{resource-based allocation}, where system energy is distributed proportionally to observable, often OS-provided, metrics such as CPU utilisation, memory usage, or I/O activity. Consequently, this approach relies on the operating system’s view of resource sharing.

    \item At \textbf{code-level granularity}, resource-based metrics are insufficient, as operating systems do not expose fine-grained utilisation within a process. As a result, two main approaches are used:

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Statistical sampling}, where execution states (e.g., call stacks) are periodically observed, and energy is attributed proportionally to the time spent in each code region,
        
        \item \textbf{Time-based attribution}, where execution intervals are explicitly delimited (e.g., via instrumentation or tracing), and energy is assigned to these intervals.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

These techniques differ in their intrusiveness and assumptions: sampling provides probabilistic approximations with limited instrumentation, while time-based approaches provide deterministic boundaries at the cost of additional instrumentation or runtime support.

\paragraph{Baseline consumption}

A recurring issue is the treatment of baseline (idle) energy consumption. Since part of the system energy is independent of the workload, tools must decide whether to:

\begin{itemize}
    \item subtract an estimated baseline,
    \item or distribute it across active entities.
\end{itemize}

This choice directly affects attribution results, particularly in low-load or shared environments where baseline consumption can dominate.

\subsection{Access to Measurement Sources}

A second challenge lies in obtaining reliable measurement and observability data. As discussed previously, profiling tools must collect two categories of information sources: hardware-level energy estimations and resource utilisation observations. Accessing these sources depends on the measurement interfaces, observability mechanisms, and privilege models exposed by the underlying platform.

\paragraph{Measurement sources} \label{measurment-sources}

Energy estimation may rely on:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{hardware-exposed interfaces}, providing energy or power readings for specific components of the system. Such interfaces may still require additional handling by the operating system or profiling tool itself, for instance to account for counter overflows or hardware-specific limitations,
    
    \item \textbf{external measurement devices}, offering higher accuracy but limited deployability and requiring time synchronisation with the attribution mechanism,
    
    \item \textbf{software-based models}, using analytical or learned models to estimate energy consumption from observable system metrics such as performance counters (i.e., hardware-exposed event counters) or operating system activity.
\end{itemize}

Similarly, resource utilisation observations may rely on operating-system activity metrics like scheduler events or runtime instrumentation frameworks.

Each source involves trade-offs between accuracy, deployability, and generality.

\paragraph{Platform dependencies}

Profiling tools are also constrained by the operating systems and execution environments they target. Many measurement interfaces, performance counters, or privilege mechanisms are operating-system specific, limiting the portability of profiling approaches across platforms.

As a result, some tools are specifically tied to a given operating-system, while others implement platform-specific backends to support multiple environments through a unified profiling interface.

\paragraph{Privilege constraints}

Access to low-level measurement and observability data is often restricted for stability and security reasons. In particular, some hardware counters and energy registers have been shown to enable side-channel attacks \todo{Cite a few attacks through RAPL and mitigations}, leading operating systems and hardware vendors to restrict or filter access to these interfaces.

\begin{itemize}
    \item hardware counters and energy registers may require elevated privileges,
    \item hardware-level metrics exposed to user-space applications may be partially filtered, aggregated, or sampled by the operating system.
\end{itemize}

As a result, profiling tools differ in how they access these sources. Some rely on operating-system-exposed interfaces available from user space. Others use privileged mechanisms to access lower-level observability facilities, such as performance counters, scheduler events, or eBPF-based instrumentation.

These design choices affect deployability, platform compatibility, profiling overhead, and the granularity of observable system activity.

\subsection{Temporal Resolution}

Energy profiling relies on periodically observing system activity and updating energy attribution estimates over time. The frequency at which these observations and attribution routines are executed directly impacts profiling quality.

Regardless of the underlying profiling mechanism increasing the observation frequency generally improves temporal precision and attribution accuracy. However, it also increases runtime overhead, data collection costs, and sensitivity to noise.

Conversely, lower observation frequencies reduce profiling overhead but may fail to capture short-lived execution phases or rapid variations in resource usage.

Temporal resolution therefore defines a trade-off between \textbf{accuracy of attribution} and \textbf{measurement overhead}.

\subsection{Profiling Overhead}

Profiling mechanisms introduce runtime overhead, which may affect the behaviour of the system being observed.

This overhead arises from \emph{(i) instrumentation of code or binaries}, \emph{(ii) periodic sampling and interrupt handling} and \emph{(iii) live logging of estimated energy shares}. Most observation mechanisms must execute online during the monitored program's execution. However, some approaches defer part of the data processing and analysis offline, reducing runtime overhead when live analysis is not required.

As a result, measurements may be biased by the profiling process itself. Minimising and quantifying this overhead is necessary to ensure the validity of observations, yet it is not always explicitly addressed.

\subsection{Virtualisation and Multi-Tenancy}

Modern execution environments introduce additional complexity through virtualisation and resource sharing.

Software execution is often structured across multiple layers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item physical hardware,
    \item hypervisors or container runtimes,
    \item virtual machines or containers,
    \item processes and threads.
\end{itemize}

This layering creates several issues:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{limited visibility}, as low-level measurements may not be accessible from within virtualised environments,
    \item \textbf{(blind) resource sharing}, where multiple entities share the same hardware components, not necessarily being aware of it,
    \item \textbf{attribution ambiguity}, particularly for the energy consumed by the virtualisation layer itself.
\end{itemize}

To address these challenges, some profiling tools expose \textbf{aggregated energy or power metrics from the host to guest environments} (e.g., via shared files or APIs). However, such approaches introduce additional dependencies on the host administrator, who must explicitly provide and expose these measurements to guest environments.

Virtualisation may also require profiling mechanisms to operate simultaneously at multiple levels (e.g., host and guest), increasing instrumentation complexity, profiling overhead, and potential inaccuracies in attribution. As a result, energy profiling in virtualised environments becomes dependent not only on measurement capabilities, but also on the visibility agreements established between host and guest environments.

\subsection{Synthesis}

These challenges highlight that software energy profiling fundamentally consists in reconstructing a mapping between \textbf{hardware-level energy measurements} and \textbf{software activity}, under partial observability and shared resource constraints.

The previous subsections show that profiling tools must simultaneously balance several competing objectives:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}, depending on the source, the frequency, the quality and precision of measurement and attribution mechanisms,
    
    \item \textbf{Deployability and generality}, depending on hardware requirements, privilege constraints, operating-system dependencies, and execution environment assumptions,
    
    \item \textbf{Granularity of attribution}, ranging from coarse process-level estimation to fine-grained code-level profiling,
    
    \item \textbf{Profiling overhead}, introduced by instrumentation, observation frequency, and online data collection,
    
    \item \textbf{Support for virtualised and multi-tenant environments}, where visibility and attribution become distributed across multiple abstraction layers.
\end{itemize}

 Improving one aspect can often impact the others. For instance, increasing attribution granularity generally requires additional instrumentation and higher observation frequencies, which increases profiling overhead. Similarly, approaches relying on privileged low-level measurements may improve accuracy while reducing portability and deployability.

Profiling tools can therefore be understood as different compromises within this multidimensional design space. The taxonomy introduced in the next section formalises these dimensions to enable structured comparison between existing approaches.

\section{Taxonomy of software energy profiling tools}

The challenges presented in Section~\ref{challenges} manifest concretely through a set of design choices implemented by profiling tools to address them. These choices reflect different compromises between attribution granularity, accuracy, deployability, overhead, and compatibility with modern infrastructures.

The objective of the proposed taxonomy is to classify profiling tools along dimensions that differentiate how they address these challenges in practice. Rather than listing profiler features, we aim to expose the underlying methodological choices that impact the behaviour, assumptions, and constraints of existing approaches. The taxonomy is the basis of the comparative classification table presented later in this paper.

To structure this design space, we organise the taxonomy around three categories of dimensions:
\emph{(i) Design choices}, \emph{(ii) Operational constraints} and \emph{(iii) Maturity and ecosystem}.

\subsection{Design choices}

Design choices correspond to the methodological and architectural decisions taken by profiling tools to observe, estimate, and attribute energy consumption. These choices directly determine the achievable granularity, accuracy, portability, and runtime cost of the profiler.

\subsubsection{Granularity}

Granularity describes the software abstraction level to which energy consumption is attributed. Profiling tools may operate at \emph{(i) virtual machine}, \emph{(ii) pod}, \emph{(iii) container}, \emph{(iv) application}, \emph{(v) process}, \emph{(vi) thread}, or \emph{(vii) code-section} level.

Process-level attribution is the most common profiling granularities and often the first step in providing other levels of granularities. These approaches generally rely on operating-system scheduling and accounting statistics, such as process CPU time or process resource utilisation, to distribute hardware-level energy consumption among processes. Their accuracy therefore depends both on the quality of the underlying operating-system resource accounting mechanisms and on the assumptions used to distribute shared hardware consumption across concurrent workloads.

Virtual-machine-, pod-, and container-level attribution are mainly designed for cloud-native and virtualised environments, where these entities represent the workload units managed by infrastructure operators and orchestration frameworks. Profilers supporting these granularities generally rely on isolation mechanisms such as cgroups or hypervisors to group processes belonging to the same workload. In practice, energy attribution is often first performed at process level, before aggregating the energy shares of the processes associated with the same virtual machine, container, or pod.

Thread-level attribution provides finer insight inside processes by attempting to distribute process-level energy consumption among threads. Achieving this granularity requires access to thread scheduling and execution statistics, generally through operating-system or runtime-level observability mechanisms.

Code-section-level profiling is more dedicated to software engineering and optimisation use cases. These approaches attempt to attribute energy consumption to functions, methods, or code regions. They typically rely on two layers of attribution: the profiler must first allocate energy consumption to the monitored process before distributing this process-level energy across internal code sections. This finer decomposition requires additional instrumentation mechanisms such as statistical call-stack sampling or execution markers. Consequently, fine-grained attribution increases runtime overhead, implementation complexity, and potentially invasiveness.

\subsubsection{Measurement sources}

Energy profiling tools differ in how they obtain measurements, but also in how they define the scope of the monitored energy consumption itself. While the monitored ``system'' theoretically corresponds to the energy consumption of the whole machine, profiling tools may instead decompose this consumption into distinct hardware domains such as CPU, DRAM, GPU, or network interfaces.

This decomposition provides insight into which hardware resources are responsible for the energy consumption attributed to software entities, which can help identifying optimisation opportunities. For instance, some applications may primarily induce CPU and memory activity, while others may be dominated by GPU computation or network transfers.

Such decomposition further allows to rely on different power and activity sources within the same profiling tool. For example, a profiler may rely on hardware-exposed interfaces for CPU and DRAM energy estimation, while simultaneously using software models for whole-system or network-related consumption. Similarly, activity attribution may rely on different sources depending on the targeted component, such as CPU utilisation metrics, memory-access counters, GPU runtime statistics, or network throughput measurements.

The taxonomy therefore distinguishes measurement sources independently for CPU, DRAM, GPU, network interfaces, and system-level consumption.

\paragraph{Power source type}

As discussed in Section~\ref{measurment-sources}, software energy profiling first requires a source of hardware-level power or energy information. Profiling tools mainly differ in whether they rely on:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{External power meters} measure physical power consumption directly using dedicated hardware instrumentation. They observe the electrical behaviour of the system itself, and provide therefore the highest measurement fidelity while remaining independent from the type of platform. They are therefore best suited for measurements validation \todo {reference a few papers doing that here}. However, they require dedicated equipment, physical access to the monitored system, and synchronisation with software activity observations. Consequently, they offer limited deployability and are difficult to integrate into cloud-native infrastructures.

    \item \textbf{Hardware exposed interfaces} rely on telemetry mechanisms implemented by hardware vendors to estimate the energy or power consumed by hardware domains such as the CPU, DRAM, GPU, or sometimes the whole system. These measurements are exposed through dedicated registers accessed via operating-system interfaces, kernel drivers, or vendor APIs. They generally provide low-overhead and high-frequency measurements, but remain strongly dependent on hardware vendors, operating systems. Additionally, because some of these interfaces have been shown to enable side-channel attacks, access to them is often subject to priviledge restriction.

    \item \textbf{Software models} estimate energy consumption indirectly from observable metrics such as TDP, processor frequency, CPU utilisation, performance counters, or other system activity metrics. Models can be analytical or trained models calibrated from reference measurements. These approaches improve portability and deployability because they can operate without specialised hardware support and remain compatible with virtualised or cloud environments where direct hardware visibility is restricted. However, they introduce modelling assumptions and calibration requirements which may reduce accuracy.
\end{itemize}

Consequently, the selected power source type strongly influences the overall profiling design space. Direct physical measurements favour accuracy but reduce deployability, while model-based approaches improve portability and scalability at the cost of additional estimation uncertainty.

\paragraph{Activity sources}

Beyond the power source itself, tools differ in the activity metrics used to attribute energy consumption to software entities. Common activity sources include CPU utilisation (derived from CPU time), hardware performance counters, scheduler statistics, memory accesses, network throughput, call stacks, or execution time markers.

Performance-counter-based approaches estimate hardware activity from lower-level architectural events such as retired instructions or cache accesses. For example, instruction counts may better reflect effective processor activity than CPU time alone, which does not capture frequency scaling or microarchitectural behaviour. These approaches may therefore improve attribution fidelity, but they increase hardware dependency, hence and may reduce portability and require privileged access.

Overall, the activity source is influenced by the desired granularity and directly impacts both attribution accuracy and portability.

\subsubsection{Attribution}

\paragraph{Activity share reconstruction}

Profiling tools differ in how they reconstruct the share of hardware activity attributable to software entities before distributing energy consumption proportionally with these shares.

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Resource-share proportional allocation} relies on observable resource usage proxies such as PID CPU utilisation percentages to estimate the share of system activity attributable to processes, containers, or virtual machines. Since these approaches leverage pre-existing scheduler statistic proxies, they are easy to implement, lightweight and therefore scalable. The downside is they rely on simplified assumptions regarding workload linearity and hardware sharing behaviour.

    \item \textbf{Statistical sampling} periodically samples execution state information such as call stacks to estimate which code regions were active during execution. The frequency of sampled execution states is then used to approximate the share of process activity attributable to functions or methods. While these approaches reduce instrumentation invasiveness, they still require access to runtime execution states and their accuracy depends on sampling frequency. 

    \item \textbf{Execution markers} explicitly delimit execution regions using instrumentation markers inserted manually or automatically into the application. Process-level energy consumption can then be distributed across the execution intervals delimited by these markers. Such approaches may provide more precise fine-grained attribution, but generally require source-code modification, bytecode instrumentation, or runtime agents hence increasing runtime overhead and potentially invasiveness.

\end{itemize}

These mechanisms reflect different trade-offs attribution precision, runtime overhead, and deployment complexity.

\paragraph{Baseline modelling and calibration}

Even when no specific application is running, computing systems still consume energy due to the operating system, background services, and idle hardware activity. Profiling tools therefore differ in whether and how they account for this baseline consumption before attribution.

Some tools explicitly model baseline energy and distribute a share of it across monitored entities according to activity indicators such as CPU utilisation or container resource allocation. Others simply attribute only the dynamically observed consumption, or do not distinguish baseline consumption at all.

Calibration is generally done in three different ways. The first consists in profiling the system at rest during a short period of time, to evaluate the idle power. In the second approach, the overall CPU utilisation is used as a proxy to estimate the ratio of active against idle power of the system. Yet another approach consists in considering the minimum power observed as the baseline.

These choices affect attribution results, especially in situations where idle or background consumption represents a significant fraction of total system power.

\subsubsection{Virtualised environment support}

Virtualisation introduces additional attribution challenges because applications running inside virtual machines generally do not have direct access to the usual hardware power sources exposed on the host system.

As a result, profiling tools supporting virtualised environments typically rely on two main approaches. Some use pretrained or calibrated models to estimate the energy share of the virtual machine from observable guest-level activity metrics such as CPU utilisation, allocated cores and memory. Others deploy monitoring components on the host system capable of estimating virtual-machine-level energy consumption, then expose these estimations to the guest environment through shared files.

Some tools targeting code-section-level attribution, rely on another profiler to obtain process-level attribution before refining the attribution within the application itself. Their support for virtualised environments therefore depends on the virtualisation support provided by the underlying profiler.

These two approaches differ in how they trade portability for accuracy and dependence on the host environment. Model-based approaches are generally more portable and do not require infrastructure operators to explicitly expose energy measurements to guest environments, but they introduce additional modelling uncertainties. Conversely, host-assisted approaches may provide more accurate estimations, at the cost of stronger dependencies on the underlying infrastructure and monitoring stack.

\subsection{Operational constraints}

Operational constraints describe the practical conditions and limitations under which profiling tools operate. They are strongly influenced by the underlying design choices and characterise the deployability, portability, and runtime implications of profiling approaches in real execution environments.

\subsubsection{Temporal resolution}

Temporal resolution represents the frequency at which measurements are collected. Higher sampling frequencies improve visibility into fast events, but increase runtime overhead, storage requirements, and sensitivity to noise.

Some tools allow temporal resolution to be configured, but the achievable range mainly depends on the underlying measurement sources. External power meters provide the highest sampling frequencies. 
Hardware-exposed interfaces usually offer a good compromise between resolution and overhead, often operating at frequencies on the order of a kilo hertz. Model-based approaches instead depend on the frequency and quality of the activity metrics used by the model.

\subsubsection{Runtime overhead}

Profiling inevitably perturbs the monitored system. Overhead mainly originates from instrumentation logic, metric collection and processing, storage operations, etc\dots

Overhead therefore constitutes a central trade-off between observability and experimental validity. It is especially determinant when monitored workloads are lightweight.

Different levers exist to reduce the overhead depending on the measurement mechanism. Common approaches include reducing the acquisition frequency, performing attribution or analysis offline rather than online, or selecting lower-overhead operating-system interfaces to access measurement sources. For example, \cite{raffinDissectingSoftwarebasedMeasurement2024} showed that different Linux interfaces used to access RAPL counters exhibit significantly different latency and energy overhead characteristics, especially at high acquisition frequencies or on idle systems.

Some tools provide mechanisms to estimate profiling overhead. However, overhead evaluation is most often reported separately in validation studies. When available, we reference these studies in the taxonomy table.

\subsubsection{Code modification}

Some profiling tools operate transparently from outside the application, while others require manual instrumentation through source-code annotations exposing execution markers, or automatic bytecode rewriting through runtime agents.

Manual instrumentation approaches, allow developers to precisely target the code sections of interest. They nevertheless have two downsides. First, developers must explicitly identify and annotate the execution regions to monitor. Second, energy consumed during the annotated execution interval is generally assumed to originate from the monitored code section itself, while other processes and system activities may still execute in parallel. Therefore these approaches are more reliable when the monitored workload is isolated as much as possible, for instance inside containers or controlled execution environments, as illustrated in \cite{kayembeExploringPrivacySecurity2025} where we used container-isolated browser automating scrcipts to estimate the cost of user interactions on email solution websites.

\subsubsection{Privilege requirements}

Tools also differ in the execution privileges required to access measurement interfaces or profiling facilities. Some approaches operate entirely in user space using standard operating-system interfaces e.g., PAPI for RAPL, whereas others require elevated privileges to access hardware counters.

Privilege requirements directly impact deployability.

\subsubsection{Platform dependency}

Profiling tools may target specific hardware architectures, operating systems, execution environments, or programming languages. Dependencies frequently arise from vendor-specific hardware-exposed energy interfaces, kernel APIs, instrumentation frameworks, or language-specific runtime mechanisms.

The taxonomy therefore distinguishes support for desktop systems, servers, single-board computers (e.g., Raspberry Pi), x86, ARM, and Apple Silicon platforms. For fine-grained code-section attribution, we additionally specify language dependencies when profiling mechanisms rely on language-specific instrumentation frameworks or runtime agents, such as Java bytecode instrumentation.

We additionally specify whether profiling tools can operate inside virtual machines or containers, and whether they provide Kubernetes integration. These aspects naturally relate to both attribution granularity and virtualised environment support. In cloud-native environments, Kubernetes manages workload scheduling and resource allocation in a role conceptually similar to that of an operating system. Energy-aware profiling at pod or container level therefore becomes particularly valuable, as it enables orchestration decisions such as scheduling or scaling to consider energy-related metrics.

\subsection{Maturity and ecosystem}

Under this dimension, we provide additional information that does not directly influence the profiling methodology itself, but reflects the maturity, usability, and practical adoption of the investigated tools.

\subsubsection{Deployment complexity}

Deployment complexity reflects the effort required to configure and deploy the profiling tools. This may include dependency installation, privilege configuration, or calibration procedures.

\subsubsection{Accuracy validation}

When available, we provide references to studies evaluating the accuracy of the profiling tools. These studies typically compare the reported measurements against external power meters or other reference measurements.

\subsubsection{Availability and licensing}

For each tool, we indicate whether it is publicly available and whether its source code is open-source.

\subsubsection{Maintenance status}

We report the presence of recent development activity as an indicator of the tool's current maintenance status.

\subsubsection{Case studies}

When available, we provide references to publicly available case studies using the profiling tools. These case studies provide additional insight into the operational contexts, capabilities, and limitations of the tools.

\subsubsection{Additional metrics}

Finally, some tools expose metrics beyond direct energy estimation, including network I/O, disk I/O, carbon-intensity estimation, or embodied-emission allocation. These additional indicators may support broader sustainability analyses but also introduce additional modelling assumptions beyond power measurement itself.


\subsection{Discussion}

The proposed taxonomy characterise the challenges introduced in the previous section into observable dimensions enabling systematic comparison between profiling approaches.

Importantly, these dimensions are strongly interdependent. For instance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item fine-grained attribution generally requires more intrusive activity sources and increases runtime overhead,
    \item support for virtualised environments constrains the visibility of measurement sources,
    \item model-based approaches improve portability but may reduce attribution accuracy,
    \item low-level observability mechanisms may require elevated privileges and reduce deployability.
\end{itemize}

Profiling tools can therefore be understood as different compromises within this multidimensional design space. The comparative analysis presented later uses this taxonomy to identify recurring architectural patterns, implicit assumptions, and trade-offs across existing approaches.

\section{Tool selection methodology}

Given the plenitude of software power and energy measurement tools, it is not practical to aim for exhaustive listing. Instead, this SoK adopts a selection methodology to identify representative and well-documented tools that reflect the main approaches and trade-offs present in the design space. The objective is not to rank tools, but rather to enable structured comparison and informed tool selection across different experimental and operational contexts.

\subsection{Seed selection}

The tool selection process is initiated by a set of initial \emph{seed tools} that are well-established in the literature or have been used in prior empirical work. These seeds reflect both traditional measurement approaches and emerging cloud-native solutions, and serve as entry points into different regions of the design space.

In particular, seed tools were selected based on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item prior use in peer-reviewed academic publications,
    \item availability of public documentation or source code,
    \item relevance to contemporary execution environments, including virtualised and containerised systems.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Inclusion criteria}

A tool will be included in the study if it satisfies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scientific grounding.} The tool is described in a peer-reviewed publication or a well-documented technical report. We want sufficient information available to understand what is measured, how measurements are obtained, and under which assumptions they are valid.
    
    \item \textbf{Software-level attribution.} The tool provides energy or power measurements attributable to software entities (e.g., system, process, container, or application), rather than reporting only raw hardware power data.
    
    \item \textbf{Relevance to modern systems.} The tool targets execution environments that are representative of current practice, including bare-metal systems, virtual machines, containers, or cloud platforms.
\end{itemize}

These criteria ensure that selected tools can be meaningfully compared along the taxonomy dimensions introduced and that their design trade-offs can be analysed.

\subsection{Exclusion criteria}

Tools were excluded from the study if they met one or more of the following conditions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hardware-only focus.} Tools limited to physical power measurement without any form of software-level attribution. \jay{This is probably redundant with Inclusion criteria "Software-level attribution"}
    
    \item \textbf{Insufficient documentation.} Tools lacking publicly available descriptions of their methodology, assumptions, or evaluation.
    
    \item \textbf{Obsolete or unsupported environments.} Tools targeting deprecated hardware platforms or software stacks with limited relevance to current systems.
    
    \item \textbf{Purely theoretical models.} Approaches proposed without an implemented tool or without empirical validation.
\end{itemize}

The application of these exclusion criteria helps avoiding representation of narrowly scoped or poorly specified approaches while maintaining focus on practical measurement tools.

\subsection{Snowballing strategy}

To mitigate bias introduced by the initial seed selection, we apply a backward and forward snowballing strategy. For each selected tool, we want to examine:

\begin{itemize}
    \item references cited in the original publication describing the tool,
    \item subsequent publications that cite the tool or build upon it,
    \item \jay{related tools discussed in comparative or evaluation studies? (Does that make sense here?)}. \todo{iness: I think so}
\end{itemize}

Candidate tools identified through snowballing are evaluated against the same inclusion and exclusion criteria. This iterative process is repeated until no additional tools meeting the criteria were identified, providing confidence that the selected set adequately covers the main classes of software energy measurement approaches discussed in the literature.

\subsection{Outcome of the selection process}

The final outcome of this selection methodology is a set of representative tools spanning different measurement approaches, attribution granularities, and execution environments. Each selected tool is characterised using a common set of features derived from the taxonomy introduced earlier, and summarised in a comparative table. This table serves as the basis for analysing trade-offs and for deriving practical decision guidelines regarding tool selection in different scenarios.
